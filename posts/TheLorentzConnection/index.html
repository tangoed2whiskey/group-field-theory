1 The algebra \mathfrak{so}(4,1)

This post will begin the journey from pure group theory towards a connection with the universe we are creating. The name ‘Lorentz connection’ is a bit of a pun, as we will be looking at how \mathfrak{so}(4,1) connects to the Lorentz algebra of symmetries in special relativity, but we won’t actually be building any spin connections or other explicit geometric structures in this post.

To start with, let’s remind ourselves of the algebra \mathfrak{so}(4,1). This was given in the first post as the algebra with generators M_{AB}, where A and B run from 0 to 4, and commutation relations

\left[M_{AB}, M_{CD}\right] = \eta_{AC} M_{BD} - \eta_{AD} M_{BC} - \eta_{BC} M_{AD} + \eta_{BD} M_{AC}, \tag{1}

where \eta_{AB}=\mathrm{diag}(-1,1,1,1,1). We will now connect this algebra back to the familiar maths of special relativity.

To start with, we will do a simple relabelling of the generators. Let’s define indices \mu and \nu that run from 0 to 3, and define J_{\mu\nu}=M_{\mu\nu} and P_\mu = M_{\mu 4}/\ell, where \ell is a constant with dimensions of length. We can now rewrite the commutation relations in terms of these new generators:

\begin{aligned} \left[J_{\mu\nu}, J_{\rho\sigma}\right] &= \eta_{\mu\rho} J_{\nu\sigma} - \eta_{\mu\sigma} J_{\nu\rho} - \eta_{\nu\rho} J_{\mu\sigma} + \eta_{\nu\sigma} J_{\mu \rho}, \\ \left[J_{\mu\nu}, P_\rho\right] &= \eta_{\mu\rho} P_\nu - \eta_{\nu\rho} P_\mu, \\ \left[P_\mu, P_\nu\right] &= \frac{1}{\ell^2} J_{\mu\nu}. \end{aligned}

Remember this is just a relabelling, none of the symmetries of the group have changed. But now we have some suggestive-shaped algebra: the first line is the algebra of Lorentz transformations, \mathfrak{so}(3,1), and the second line says that the P_\mu transform as a four-vector under Lorentz transformations. This line is also familiar from the Poincaré algebra of special relativity. The third line, however, is different from special relativity: it says that two translations do not commute, but instead give a rotation. This is a key feature of the algebra \mathfrak{so}(4,1), and it will have important consequences for the physics that emerges from this algebra. We write the separation of the algebra into these two parts as \mathfrak{so}(4,1) = \mathfrak{so}(3,1) + \mathfrak{p}, where \mathfrak{p} is the part of the algebra generated by the P_\mu. The group corresponding to the algebra \mathfrak{p} is called the de Sitter group, and is represented as the quotient group \mathrm{dS}_4=\mathrm{SO}(4,1)/\mathrm{SO}(3,1).

We can think of the non-commuting of translations like the behaviour of translations on a sphere: if you start at the north pole and translate down to the equator, then translate along the equator, and then translate back up to the north pole, you will be facing in a different direction than you started. This is a consequence of the curvature of the sphere, and it implies that the universe we are building also has a built-in curvature.

If we take the limit \ell \to \infty, the commutation relations reduce to those of the Poincaré algebra, which is the algebra of symmetries of flat spacetime in special relativity. This means that the algebra \mathfrak{so}(4,1) can be thought of as a ‘deformation’ of the Poincaré algebra, where the deformation parameter is \ell.

2 Casimir invariants in our new basis

The Casimir invariants of \mathfrak{so}(4,1) can also be rewritten in terms of the new generators. Recall that there is a second order and a fourth order Casimir invariant. Replacing the A, B indices with \mu, \nu and the 4 index with P_\mu gives us

\mathcal{C}_2^{\mathfrak{so}(4,1)} = \frac{1}{2} \eta^{\mu\rho} \eta^{\nu\sigma} J_{\mu\nu} J_{\rho\sigma} + \ell^2 \eta^{\mu\nu} P_\mu P_\nu=\mathcal{C}_2^\mathrm{Lorentz} + \ell^2 P^2,

where \mathcal{C}_2^\mathrm{Lorentz} is the Casimir invariant of the Lorentz algebra \mathfrak{so}(3,1), and P^2 = \eta^{\mu\nu} P_\mu P_\nu. The fourth order Casimir invariant is given by

\mathcal{C}_4^\mathfrak{so}(4,1) = \eta_{\mu \nu} W^\mu W^\nu + \left(\tilde{\mathcal{C}}_2^\mathrm{Lorentz}\right)^2,

where \tilde{\mathcal{C}}_2^\mathrm{Lorentz}=\frac{1}{8} \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} J_{\mu\nu} J_{\rho\sigma} is the dual of the second order Casimir invariant of the Lorentz algebra, and W^\mu = \frac{1}{8} \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} J_{\nu\rho} P_\sigma is the Pauli-Lubanski vector from special relativity. So although our \mathfrak{so}(4,1) algebra is definitely 5-dimensional, it contains the familiar structures of special relativity within it. In the next post we will introduce the concept of fields on our group, and we will begin to connect our Casimir invariants and representations to those fields to construct the beginnings of a physcial theory.
